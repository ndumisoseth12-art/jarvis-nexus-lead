The product in 60 seconds

NSM is an AI automation agency for South African small businesses. You sell a bundle of bots that runs the boring-but-important admin work a small business owner usually drops the ball on — chasing no-shows, getting paid, replying to reviews, waking up customers who went quiet, asking happy ones for referrals.

You sell it in three ways:

Industry packs (Salon, Airbnb, Recovery, Healthcare) — pre-bundled 3-bot kits for a specific kind of business, R2,500 setup + R1,990/month. This is the main sales motion.

Operator tier — pick any 5 bots, mid-priced. For businesses that already know what they need.

Command tier — the full 11-bot suite. Top-tier, fewer buyers but higher LTV.

The bots are built and live. The portal is up. The only thing missing is paying clients — which means the bottleneck is sales, not code.

How one customer event flows through the system

Walk through with one realistic example — a salon client's customer doesn't show up for their appointment.

1. Salon's booking system marks "Boitumelo" as a no-show.
↓ webhook fires
2. B3 No-Show Rescue wakes up. Logs the booking in B3's Google Sheet.
↓ immediately
3. Groq writes a polite rebook email in the salon's voice.
↓
4. Gmail sends the email to Boitumelo with a rebook link.
↓ in parallel
5. Telegram pings the salon owner: "Boitumelo no-show, rescue email sent."
↓ 3 days pass
6. B3 daily scanner sees no rebook. Sends Touch 2 (warmer "still here if you want it").
↓ 7 days from start, still no rebook
7. Sends Touch 3 (final "we'll keep your file open one more week").
↓ next morning
8. B8 Owner Brief at 7am tells the salon owner: "Yesterday: 1 rebook recovered (R650), 1 still pending."
↓ Monday morning
9. B14 Bot Health Brief tells you: "B3 fired 3 sequences this week, all 3 parsed cleanly."

Every bot follows a similar pattern: event happens → bot reads sheet → AI writes message → email/telegram sent → sheet updated. The complexity is just multiple bots doing this in parallel for multiple clients.

Your daily routine

What to actually check. What to ignore. The bots run themselves — you mostly read summaries.

  1. Morning (5 min) · Check TelegramAny Parse Fail Alerts? Any unhappy-customer alerts from B6? Any VIP fanouts from B2/B4? If nothing scary in Telegram, your bots had a normal night.
  2. Once a week (Monday morning) · Read B14Your B14 Bot Health Brief lands at 6am SAST. Skim it. Any silent bots? Any prompt versions flagged amber/red? If everything's green, close the email and get on with your day.
  3. Whenever you sign a new client · Connect their sheetsThe bots are single-tenant right now — one Google Sheet per bot. When you onboard a new client, you'll duplicate the sheet, update the sheet ID in their workflow, and import. Phase 3 work will make this multi-tenant.
  4. When Paystack KYC clears · Flip B12/B13 to live modeRight now billing is sandbox. Once your Paystack account is approved, open /golive and follow the runbook (Paystack keys + webhook + flip the subscribe button) to take real payments.
  5. Never · Manually edit a Google Sheet headerThe bots match columns by name. If you rename or add a stray space ("Email " vs "Email"), things break silently. If you really need to add a column, do it at the END of row 1 and update the workflow schema too.

Setting up the bots for a new client

The honest walkthrough. Each pilot/client takes you ~45 minutes the first time, dropping to ~20 minutes once you have a routine.

Architecture honesty: the bots are currently single-tenant — built for one business. To onboard a new client, you duplicate the workflow + sheets and point them at that client's data. This is heavy by design (works fine for the first 3–10 clients) and will become a proper multi-tenant system at ~15 clients. Don't try to fix it before then.
⭐ The easy way — generate the client's page + workflow files in 2 minutes.

Open a terminal in the project folder (JarvisNexus) and run:
node kit/new-client.js
It asks plain-English questions — business name, industry (salon / trade / trainer / airbnb / healthcare / recovery), owner email, phone, brand colour, and an optional price list — then builds everything into kit/output/<web-name>/:
· intake.html — the client's branded landing page. Double-click it to preview in your browser, then deploy it.
· workflows/ — all 12 bot JSONs, pre-filled with the client's name, owner email and webhook path.
· SETUP_NOTES.md — a per-client checklist that mirrors the steps below.

You never edit a config file by hand. The wizard does the typing. The numbered steps below are the n8n wiring you still do yourself (sheets, Telegram, activation) — the wizard deliberately leaves the Google Sheet IDs blank for you to paste in at step 4.

Before the demo call: nothing. Just talk to them, use the pack page that fits their business as a visual aid.

After they sign the pilot agreement, you do this once per client:

  1. Gather their setup info (one quick email)Ask the client for: their business name (exactly how they want it shown to customers), their owner email (where alerts will go), a phone number you'll use to create a dedicated Telegram chat for them, their booking system URL if any (Cal.com, Fresha, Vagaro), the list of their existing customers if they have one (CSV export from their POS or spreadsheet).
  2. Create their Google Sheets (duplicate your templates)In your Google Drive, find each template sheet you use (B3 Bookings, B4 Invoices, etc. — only the ones included in their pack). Right-click → Make a copy → rename it "{Client Name} · B3 Bookings", etc. Move them into a new folder called "Clients / {Client Name}". Share each one with the client's owner email (Editor access) and your own. Copy the sheet ID from each URL — you'll need them in step 4.
  3. Create their Telegram chatIn Telegram, create a new group named "{Client Name} · NSM alerts". Add the client + yourself. Send any message in the chat, then visit api.telegram.org/bot<YOUR_BOT_TOKEN>/getUpdates in a browser — find the chat ID for the new group (it'll be a negative number like -1001234...). Save it; you'll paste it into n8n in step 4.
  4. Duplicate the workflows in n8nFor each bot in their pack: open the existing workflow → click the "..." menu → Duplicate → rename "B3 No-Show Rescue · {Client Name}". In the duplicate, open each Google Sheets node and change the Document URL to the client's sheet from step 2. Open each Telegram node and change the chatId to the new group from step 3. Open each Gmail node and update the recipient/owner-email field. Open the "Edit Fields — Setup" node (where it exists) and update businessName + ownerEmail + ownerFirstName fields. Shortcut: if you ran node kit/new-client.js, import the ready-made JSONs from kit/output/<web-name>/workflows/ instead of duplicating by hand — the client's name, owner email and webhook path are already filled in, so you only paste in each Google Sheet ID.
  5. Test one bot end-to-end before activating the restPick the simplest bot in their pack (usually B3 No-Show Rescue or B11 Review Pro). Fire a test submission at it — easiest is node kit/test-form.js <web-name>, which sends a clearly-marked "NSM TEST" entry for you and tells you if it's connected (or use the n8n "Execute Workflow" button). Confirm: email lands in their inbox, Telegram alert lands in the new group, sheet row appears. Once one bot works end-to-end, the pattern repeats — the others will work too.
  6. Activate all their workflowsToggle each one to Active. The scheduled bots (B2/B8/B10/B14) will fire at their next cron window. The event-triggered bots (B3/B4/B6/B7/B9/B11) wait for real customer events from the client's booking system or webhook.
  7. Hand over the client onboarding docSend the client a short email with: their portal links, what the daily 7am B8 brief looks like, who to contact if something seems wrong (you), and the day-25 check-in date for the pilot conversion conversation.
🔒 Golden rule — test before you hand over.

Never give a client their form link until you've proven it works. Once their workflow is activated, run:
node kit/test-form.js <web-name>
It fires a clearly-marked "NSM TEST" submission at the client's webhook and tells you straight:
· ✅ CONNECTED & LIVE — then confirm the test row landed in the Sheet + the Telegram alert + the email. If all three arrived, it's safe to hand over.
· ❌ NOT LIVE — the workflow isn't activated yet (or the webhook path is wrong). Fix it in n8n and re-test.
· ⚠️ CONNECTED BUT ERRORED — usually a missing Sheet ID; check the workflow's Executions tab in n8n.

One catch: test-form.js runs from your laptop (server-side), so it proves the bot is reachable but can't see a browser's CORS check. After it goes green, also open the live page and submit the form once from your phone or browser — that's the only way to prove the whole chain works for a real customer. (The client webhooks are already CORS-enabled so a standalone page works; if you ever host the form under the main portal instead, its strict security policy blocks the direct call — post through the /api/webhook/ proxy there.)

This one habit means a customer never sees a broken form in front of your client.
Gotchas you'll only learn the hard way (so I'm telling you now):
· When you duplicate a workflow in n8n, the credentials stay attached to the original. Sometimes the duplicate doesn't inherit them — re-pick the credential in each Google Sheets / Gmail / Telegram / Header Auth node if needed.
· Google Sheets column names with stray spaces (e.g. "Email " vs "Email") silently break things. Always copy templates exactly, never retype headers.
· Test by firing one webhook end-to-end before activating the whole suite. It catches 90% of setup mistakes before they reach the client.

How you sell this

The product is done. The next 90 days are about humans, not code.

📋 The full sales playbook is ready — and shareable. Buyer psychology (who you’re really selling to, their hidden objections), the universal script flow, WhatsApp / phone / high-ticket-closer versions, the follow-up sequence, plus every per-pack cold email, DM, walk-in opener and objection handler — all on a gated page you can send your team: jarvis-nexus-lead-jkon.vercel.app/outreach (passphrase LoveGod12). Every script has a one-click Copy button.
  1. Step 1 · Run 3 free 30-day pilotsOne salon, one Airbnb host, one accountant (or healthcare practice). OUTREACH.md has the cold-email scripts already written for each. Send the first one tomorrow.
  2. Step 2 · Day 25 of each pilot, ask for a testimonial"Was this useful enough to keep paying for?" If yes → convert to R1,990/month using PILOT_AGREEMENT.md. If no → ask for one honest paragraph about what worked. That paragraph goes on your pack pages.
  3. Step 3 · With 2–3 testimonials live, start outreach round 2Your cold emails now reference real client results. Conversion rate roughly 5–10x. This is when you start charging from day 1 instead of running free pilots.
  4. Step 4 · At 5 paying clients, hire one virtual assistant for client setupYou stop being the person who manually duplicates sheets and updates IDs. You stay the person who closes sales and tunes prompts.
  5. Step 5 · At 15 paying clients, build the multi-tenant versionThat's when one-bot-per-client stops scaling. Until then, the manual approach is fine and faster than the "right" architecture.
Why pilots before paid: nobody buys an SMB tool from a 23-year-old solo founder with zero clients. Real testimonials buy you the credibility that your product alone can't.

The money side

What you charge. What a sale looks like end-to-end. What your costs are.

Pricing: R2,500 once-off setup + R1,990/month. Industry packs and Operator tier are both at this price. Command tier (all 11 bots) costs more — set it when you have a buyer who needs everything.

What a sale looks like:

Prospect lands on a pack page (e.g. salon.html) → clicks "Book Demo" → lands on your Cal.com → you do a 20-minute call → if interested, you send them the PILOT_AGREEMENT.md → 30-day free pilot starts → day 25 you ask for the upgrade → if they say yes, you send the Paystack subscribe link → they're paying R1,990/month from there forward.

Turning billing on: Paystack is in sandbox until your KYC is approved. The day it clears, open /golive (gated, passphrase below) and follow the plain-English runbook — it walks you through the Paystack keys, the webhook, and flipping the subscribe button live, step by step.

Your monthly costs (per client roughly):

Groq AI calls: ~R30–R80 depending on volume. Railway n8n hosting: ~R200 flat (covers all your clients). Google Sheets + Gmail + Telegram: R0. So your margin per client is roughly R1,700–R1,950 after AI costs, minus your share of the Railway flat fee.

What you keep: at 10 paying clients, that's roughly R18,000–R19,500/month gross profit. At 20 clients, about R38,000–R40,000/month. The economics get good fast because the bots don't cost more to run when you have more clients.

Honesty note: these are unit economics, not promises. You haven't sold to anyone paid yet. The numbers only become real after the first 3 pilots convert.

Your brand — logo, colours, and the kit

NSM stands for Neural Systems Management. Use the full name on first contact (homepage, email signatures, legal, business cards); use just NSM everywhere else. The tagline is Operations, Automated. — always with the capital A and the full stop.

The logo

The logo is the NSM wordmark — with a gradient sweep underline that ends in the period from "Operations, Automated." There is no separate icon or square; the old rotated-diamond mark is retired — don't bring it back. On a browser tab or app icon it shrinks to just N. on an indigo tile.

Colours

#818cf8 glow #6366f1 indigo #4f46e5 deep #08080c warm-black

The indigo gradient (glow → indigo → deep) is the signature, on warm-black surfaces. Green / amber / red are for status only (good / warning / bad) — never decoration.

Fonts

Inter — body, buttons, UI. Instrument Serif (italic) — headlines, hero words, big numbers. JetBrains Mono — labels, IDs, timestamps, the // comment markers.

The brand kit — where the files live

portal/_brand/

These are your source files for when you need to hand a logo to a printer, a directory, or a social profile. The live website doesn't read them (its logo is baked into the pages), so nothing breaks if you move the folder.

  • nsm-logo-c.svg — master logo, for dark backgrounds.
  • nsm-logo-c-light.svg — for white / light backgrounds (dark ink).
  • nsm-logo-c-mono.svg — single colour, for print / embroidery / stamps.
  • nsm-mark.svg — compact wordmark, no subtext, for tight spaces.
  • nsm-favicon.svg + nsm-favicon-*.png — the "N." icon for browser tabs, app stores, social avatars (1024 biggest → 32 smallest).
  • nsm-business-card.html — print-ready card. Open in Chrome → Print → Save as PDF → hand to a printer.
  • preview.html — shows the 3 original concepts with C chosen. Reference only.
Brand golden rules:
· Never recolour the logo — the indigo gradient is fixed. The only single-colour version is nsm-logo-c-mono.svg.
· On light backgrounds use the -light file, never the dark master.
· The full stop after NSM is part of the logo — it ties to "Automated." Keep it.
· The picture that appears when you paste a link (WhatsApp, LinkedIn) is portal/og-cover.png — it's rendered from og-cover.html. If the brand ever changes, update the HTML and re-screenshot it at 1200×630.
