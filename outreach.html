// Section 01

Who you’re really selling to

Not “a business”. One tired owner-operator who built this with their hands, wears every hat, and quietly knows money is leaking out the back — they just can’t watch all of it at once.

The emotional need

Relief, and the feeling of being on top of it. They don’t want software — they want to stop carrying the unreplied review, the client who never came back, the invoice they never chased, all in their head at once.

The identity they want

To be a real, professional business — not a hustle, not someone scrambling. Someone whose customers feel looked-after even when the owner is flat out.

The risk they’re avoiding

Being made a fool of again. They’ve paid for a booking app / “marketing guy” / agency that did nothing. And the deeper fear: tech that embarrasses them in front of their own customers.

The outcome they secretly hope for

That it just… works. Quietly, in the background, making them look good and on top of things — without more work for them.

What they’re thinking but not saying

  • “This kid is 23 — is he going to disappear in three months?” (your age is both the disarm and the doubt — address it head-on.)
  • “AI will sound fake and make me look amateur to people who know me.”
  • “I’ll have to learn something complicated or change my whole system.”
  • “Another monthly fee I’ll forget to cancel.”
  • “If this is free, what’s the catch? What are you taking?”

Why they say yes (the actual mechanism)

  • Risk reversal — “free, nothing owed” unclenches the wallet. Their money is personal; this is the only frame that gets past it.
  • Being understood — you name their pain more precisely than they would. “This person actually gets it.”
  • “In your voice” — kills the fake-AI fear instantly.
  • “I do all the setup” — kills the effort fear.
  • Disarming candour — 23, Joburg, honest about what it can’t do yet. Trust through honesty, not polish.

Per-segment nuance (same buyer, different pressure point)

  • Salon: a no-show feels like personal disrespect and a dead chair. IG-native, relationship-driven. Lead with the rebook + their rating.
  • Airbnb: Superhost status anxiety + time-poverty. Review timing literally is money. Lead with “Superhost stays Superhost while you sleep.”
  • Recovery / B2B: writing off money feels like personal failure; they’re tone-sensitive and allergic to “debt collector.” Lead with “your polite voice, flat fee, no % cut.”
  • Healthcare: POPIA + clinical-system fear dominates everything. Lead with “doesn’t touch your PMS, admin data only, you own it.” Earn trust before you sell outcomes.
// Section 02

Emotional triggers & hidden objections

Pull these levers — honestly. Every one is grounded in something true about the product.

Relief

“You don’t have to hold all of this in your head anymore.” The dominant feeling you’re selling. Lead here.

Loss made concrete

Loss aversion — but with real numbers. “~R6,000/mo already walking out the door” lands harder than any feature, and it’s true.

Pride / professionalism

Replies in their voice. They look more on-top-of-it to their own customers, not less human.

Control

The 7am brief = they start every day already knowing. Owners crave the feeling of a cockpit.

Belonging

“Built by someone like me, here in Joburg.” Anti-corporate, anti-overseas-SaaS. They’d rather back a local who gets it.

Reciprocity

You give first (free pilot, you do the setup). The instinct to reciprocate is strong once it’s clearly worked.

Hidden objections → how you defuse each (before they raise it)

  • “You’ll disappear / this is a side project.”Name it first: “I’m 23 and building this full-tilt from Joburg — that’s exactly why I do all the setup myself and why I’m only taking 3 pilots: so each one actually gets my attention.”
  • “AI will sound fake / embarrass me.”“Every message goes out in your voice, and you approve the first one for tone before anything sends. You’ll see exactly what your customers see.”
  • “It’s complicated / I’ll have to change my system.”“You change nothing. I wire it into your Gmail and a Google Sheet you already understand. If you can read an email, you can run it.”
  • “Another fee I’ll forget to cancel.”“Free for 30 days, no card. At day 25 you look at what it recovered and decide. If it didn’t earn its keep, you owe nothing and we stop.”
  • “If it’s free, what’s the catch?”“The catch is honest: I need 3 real results I can point to. You get 30 days free; if it works, I earn a client and a testimonial. That’s the whole deal.”
// Section 03

The universal script (the flow)

This is the spine under every channel. Don’t recite it — internalise the moves, then talk like a human. The order matters: earn the right to each step before the next.

  1. Opening hook — disarm, don’t pitch

    Pattern-interrupt the “here comes a sale” reflex.

    “Hi — I’m Seth. I’m not selling you anything today. I’m 23, building a small thing for SA [salons/practices/hosts], and I just want to ask you one question.”

  2. Rapport questions — genuine, short

    Two questions, real curiosity. Let them talk.

    “How long have you run [place]? Is it just you, or do you have a team?”

  3. Discovery — make them describe the pain

    Don’t tell them the problem. Ask, and let them say it in their words (this becomes their testimonial later).

    “When a [client doesn’t show / review lands / invoice goes overdue] — walk me through what actually happens right now. Do you chase it? Or does it just slip?”

  4. Problem amplification — gently, with their number

    Reflect the cost back. Remove blame — it’s not that they don’t care, it’s that they’re at the chair.

    “So that’s maybe R[X] a month? And it’s not that you don’t care — it’s that you can’t be at the chair AND chasing rebooks at the same time. Nobody can.”

  5. Future pacing — let them feel the relief

    Paint the specific Monday morning.

    “Picture Monday: one email at 7am, two minutes, and you already know what got recovered over the weekend and the one client to call. The rest ran while you slept.”

  6. The ask — low-stakes, specific

    Small yes, not a big one.

    “I’m taking 3 businesses on a free 30-day pilot. No card, I do all the setup. Want to be one of them?”

  7. Objection handling — answer the hidden one

    Whatever they say, hear the fear underneath (see Section 02) and answer that, not just the words.

// Section 06

Founder principles

The rules that keep outreach honest, legal and effective. Read once before sending.

Strategic frame: this is pilot outreach, not paid outreach. You need 3–4 real testimonials before paid cold outreach converts. Posture until then: free 30-day pilot, then R2,500 setup + R1,990/mo if it worked.

Before sending

  • Personalise every message — name, suburb, a recent review, the listing. Never blast.
  • Business hours only — Tue/Wed 10–11am email; weekday mornings DM. Never nights/weekends.
  • One channel at a time — email OR DM OR walk-in. All three at once is harassment.
  • Link to the relevant pack page, never the homepage.

Never do

  • Fake social proof (“100s of clients”, fake testimonials, “trusted by”).
  • AI-buzzword bingo (“AI revolution”, “10x your revenue”, “digital transformation”).
  • Promise WhatsApp delivery — Phase 3. Email + SMS only right now.
  • Undercut your price — pilots are free OR R1,990, nothing between.
  • Cold-WhatsApp strangers — POPIA + WhatsApp ToS forbid it. Warm replies only.
  • Mass-CC or BCC. Always 1:1.
Cadence: Day 0 first contact · Day 3–5 soft bump · Day 10–14 final bump · Day 14+ stop, move to “check back in 90 days.”
POPIA hygiene: every cold email ends with “Reply STOP and I won’t follow up.” Publicly-listed business contact info only — never scrape personal data. Keep a do-not-contact list and honour it.
